Direction A · Ledger

Borrowed from the instrument your buyer already trusts — the ruled ledger. Columns, hairline rules and tabular figures. The hero reconciles: what it costs you now on the left, what it becomes on the right, ruled off with a total.

Type inverts the usual pairing — grotesque headlines over a serif body, which reads considered rather than startup-generic. The only warm colour is a single brass note, and debit-red / credit-green appear exactly where an accountant expects them.

Says: we are precise, and we understand your month-end. Risk: the quietest of the three — it needs disciplined photography and real numbers to stay from feeling austere.

Direction B · Routes

The structural device is the route your goods and money actually take — supplier, warehouse, branch, rep, customer, cash. It runs as a spine down the page on a phone and turns horizontal on a wide screen, with each product tagged to the stage it covers.

That solves the positioning problem directly: instead of nineteen services in a list, a buyer sees exactly where you plug into their operation. Deep teal ground with a signal orange — confident and East African without reaching for any of the usual imagery.

Says: we understand how your business actually moves. Risk: the warmest and most opinionated — it commits hardest to a colour.

Direction C · Control Room

The most direct answer to "prove you build software": the hero is the product. A live group-overview panel with real branch names, stock at cost, and status pills that read at a glance. Figures tick quietly so it feels connected rather than screenshotted.

Semantic colour does the work — green in stock, amber running low, red out — kept deliberately separate from the cyan accent, which is the discipline that stops a dashboard turning into confetti.

Says: we are engineers and this is real. Risk: dark technical UI is the most common look in software marketing, so it has the least differentiation working in its favour — it wins on execution, not novelty.
